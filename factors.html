What is Factor Investing?

Factor investing is an investing strategy that uses quantifiable criteria to explain returns on investments. The two main types of factors are macroeconomic factors which explain the broad market and style factors which help to explain returns within asset classes. Macroeconomic factors would include interest and unemployment rates. Style factors would include size, value, volatility, quality, and momentum. Style factors are the factors investment funds concentrate on.

The 5 Style Factors

  1. Size - Size deals with market capitalization of a company
  2. Value - The value factor is concerned with the price of a stock in relation to its fundamental value. Ratios such as price to book, price to earnings, or price to enterprise value are used.
  3. Momentum - Momentum looks at the price movement of an equity. Stocks prices that trend up tend to stay moving up and vice versa.
  4. Quality - Quaity looks at a company's quantitative financial metrics in addition to qualitative metrics such as good management.
  5. volatility - Stocks that have less volatility, as measured by standard deviation, in their price are believed to offer better returns.

The Fama-French Three-Factor Model

In the 1990s, economists Eugene Fama and Kenneth French released a paper that indicated that size and value explained a statistically significant portion of market returns. Along with size and value, Fama and French used excess market returns due to market risk as the third factor.

There are indices based around these factors such as the Russell 2000 Value index. According to data from Dimensional Fund Advisors , small cap value has outperformed the S&P 500 in every 20-year period from 1930-2019. So why not put everything into small cap value?
